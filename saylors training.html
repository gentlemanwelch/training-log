Reference

What every word means

The training plan uses specific terms. Learn them once and the workouts will make sense for the whole summer.

Easy run~75-80% effort
Conversational pace. You should be able to talk in full sentences while running. If you can't, you're going too fast — slow down even more. This is the pace runners get wrong most often. For you, probably 9:30-10:30 per mile. Walk if you need to. Easy means easy.
Steady run~82-87% effort
Comfortable but focused. You can still talk but only in short phrases. You're working but not suffering. Probably 8:30-9:00 per mile.
Tempo run~88-92% effort
Comfortably hard. One-word answers only. You're focused, breathing rhythmically, and it takes real effort to hold the pace. Probably 7:30-8:00 per mile.
Intervals / Reps~93-97% effort
Hard but controlled. Short bursts of fast running with rest in between. Not all-out sprints — you should be able to repeat the pace evenly across all reps. Each interval has a target time.
StridesQuick & smooth
4×20-second strides means: run 4 times, each for 20 seconds, with full rest in between. Each stride is relaxed fast running, not a sprint — about 90% of your top speed with smooth form. Walk or stand for 30-60 seconds between each one. They build leg speed without taxing your aerobic system. Always do them on a flat, smooth surface.
Warm-up / Cool-downBookends every workout
Before any hard workout: 10 minutes of very easy jogging, then some leg swings and dynamic stretches. After: 10 more minutes of easy jogging to flush the legs. Never skip these — they're how you stay healthy.
400m / 800mDistances on a track
1 lap on a standard track = 400 meters (about ¼ mile). 800m = 2 laps. 1 mile ≈ 4 laps. Most of the harder workouts happen on the track because the distance is exact and the surface is fast.
Progression runEasy → steady
Start at easy pace, gradually pick it up, finish at steady pace. The point is to practice running faster when you're already tired — which is exactly what happens in the second mile of a 2-mile race.
Time trialRace against the clock
An all-out effort over a set distance to measure where your fitness actually is. There are two in this plan: a 1-mile time trial in Week 6 (to predict your 2-mile fitness) and a 2-mile time trial in Week 10 (the real test).
Goal paceWhat sub-13 actually requires
For a 13:00 two-mile, you need 6:30 per mile, which is 1:37.5 per 400m lap. Goal-pace workouts get your body familiar with that exact speed so it feels normal on race day.

Strength routine

Do this on any two days that aren't track-workout days. After an easy run is ideal, or as its own session. Bodyweight only. The strongest injury-prevention you can do.

Bodyweight Squats

3 × 12 reps

Feet shoulder-width. Sit back like there's a chair behind you. Keep your chest up. Go to about 90 degrees at the knee.

Walking Lunges

3 × 10 each leg

Step forward, knee tracks over toes (not past). Front thigh roughly parallel to ground. Push through the front heel to stand.

Single-Leg Balance

3 × 30 seconds each

Stand on one foot. Try with eyes closed once you can do it easily. Builds ankle stability — critical for road and trail running.

Plank

3 × 30-45 seconds

Elbows under shoulders, body straight from head to heels. No sagging hips, no piked butt. Core strength = better running form when tired.

Glute Bridges

3 × 15 reps

Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat. Squeeze glutes and lift hips to make a straight line from knees to shoulders. Hold 2 seconds at the top.

Calf Raises

3 × 15 reps

Stand on the edge of a step. Lower heels below the step, then rise up onto your toes. Slow on the way down — that's where the strength gets built.

Side Plank

3 × 20-30 seconds each side

On one elbow, body in a straight line. Hips up, don't let them sag toward the floor. Hits the hip stabilizers runners always neglect.

Single-Leg Glute Bridge

2 × 10 each leg

Glute bridge but with one leg extended. Drive through the heel of the planted leg. Hardest exercise here — work up to it.

Non-negotiables

The rules that matter

Speed comes from training. Staying fast comes from these.

01

Easy days are easy

If your heart rate goes above ~170 on an easy day, you're running too hard. Slow down. Walk if you need to. The point isn't to feel tired today — it's to be fresh for the workout tomorrow.

02

Two rest days. Always.

Rest days aren't optional and they aren't "active recovery." They're full days off. This is when your body actually gets stronger — not during the runs.

03

Pain isn't toughness

If something hurts for more than two runs in a row — shin, knee, hip, foot — stop and tell Dad. Stress fractures start as small pain you push through. Smart runners stop early.

04

Fuel like a runner

You're training harder than last summer — you have to eat more than last summer. Carbs before workouts, protein at meals, no skipped meals. Under-fueling is the #1 silent killer of teenage girl runners.

05

Sleep is training

Aim for 9 hours. The adaptation from a hard workout happens during sleep. Bad sleep = workouts don't count.

06

Shoes matter

Get a real pair of running shoes (Brooks Ghost, Saucony Ride, Nike Pegasus — around $130). Old basketball shoes or tennis shoes will give you shin splints fast.

07

Pace is a skill

Going out too fast is the most common mistake. On a 2-mile, you want your second mile to be roughly the same speed as your first. Learning that takes practice.

08

Show up honestly

Some days you'll feel terrible and bail on a run. Some days you'll feel great. Log both. The data only helps you if it's real.
